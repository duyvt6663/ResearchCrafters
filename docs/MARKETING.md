# ResearchCrafters Marketing and GTM

Last updated: 2026-05-07

## 1. Positioning

ResearchCrafters is the practice platform for AI research engineering.

Short positioning:

- CodeCrafters for research papers.
- Learn famous AI papers by replaying the decisions behind them.
- Build research taste through implementation, experiments, evidence, and writing.

Primary promise:

You do not truly understand a paper until you can make its decisions, survive its failed
branches, implement its core idea, and defend its evidence.

## 2. Category

ResearchCrafters should avoid positioning as "paper summaries" or "AI tutor." Those
categories are crowded and low-status.

Better category:

- Research engineering practice.
- Executable paper training.
- Research taste gym.

The product should feel rigorous, slightly difficult, and high-status. It should not feel
like passive education.

## 3. Audience

Best initial audience:

- AI engineers who read papers but do not feel research-fluent.
- Strong software engineers entering ML and LLM systems.
- Senior engineers who want deeper taste than "I can call an API."
- Graduate students who need structured reps outside lab apprenticeship.

Do not start with pure academics as the only audience. The reachable market is larger
among engineers who want research depth but do not have a lab environment.

## 4. Core Message

Primary headline options:

- Rebuild the research behind famous AI papers.
- Learn AI papers by surviving the decisions that made them work.
- Stop reading papers passively. Replay the research.
- The research engineering gym for serious AI builders.

Supporting copy:

- Each package turns a famous paper into an executable journey: decisions, failed
  branches, implementation tasks, experiments, evidence, and writing.
- You work in your own editor, run real tests, inspect real evidence, and get mentor-style
  feedback.
- The hard part is not reading the final result. The hard part is choosing what to try
  when the answer is not known yet.

## 5. Differentiation

Compared with paper summaries:

- ResearchCrafters asks the learner to decide, implement, test, and write.

Compared with Kaggle:

- ResearchCrafters trains research process and paper-level reasoning, not only leaderboard
  optimization.

Compared with CodeCrafters:

- ResearchCrafters is inspired by the staged build-and-test loop, but the unit is a
  research paper and its hidden branch graph, not a production system clone.

Compared with courses:

- ResearchCrafters is not a lecture sequence. It is a case-based practice environment.

Compared with ARA:

- ARA is an agent-native research artifact protocol. ResearchCrafters turns that artifact
  into a human learning journey with tests, decisions, rubrics, and mentor feedback.

## 6. Viral Hook

The share loop should expose a specific gap in the learner's understanding without making
the product feel unserious.

Shareable moments:

- "I thought I understood FlashAttention until I had to choose the bottleneck."
- "I picked the branch 72% of engineers pick. It was wrong."
- "My research-thinking score: implementation strong, evidence interpretation weak."
- "This paper's most important failed path was not in the paper."

Share card elements:

- Paper/package title.
- Completion status.
- Research-thinking profile.
- Hardest decision.
- Most common wrong branch the learner avoided or selected.
- One evidence-grounded insight written by the learner.

Avoid vanity-only gamification. The share artifact should signal serious skill.

## 7. Content-Led Acquisition

Each ERP can produce many public content pieces:

- One decision poll.
- One failed-branch explainer.
- One implementation trap.
- One evidence-interpretation challenge.
- One "what the paper does not tell you" post.
- One short benchmark or visualization.

Example post template:

```text
You're trying to make exact attention faster for long contexts.

Which constraint do you attack first?
A. Reduce FLOPs
B. Reduce HBM traffic
C. Cache more aggressively
D. Approximate attention

Most people choose the obvious answer. The paper's key move is more subtle.
```

## 8. Launch Plan

Pre-launch:

- Build a public waitlist page around one flagship package.
- Publish 5-10 decision posts from that package.
- Interview target users with the package outline.
- Recruit a small alpha cohort of strong AI engineers.

Alpha:

- Release one free onboarding package and one flagship paid package.
- Offer founder pricing or lifetime access to early users.
- Track confusion at the stage level.
- Manually talk to users who finish or churn.

Beta:

- Add second package.
- Add share cards.
- Add team trial for small AI engineering teams.
- Start weekly public "research decision challenge" posts.

Launch:

- Position around the flagship package, not the broad platform.
- Show concrete examples of branches, tests, and evidence.
- Use serious testimonials from engineers and researchers.

## 9. Pricing

Individual:

- Free: onboarding package and first 1-2 stages of each package.
- Pro: full package access, mentor feedback, code examples, result history.
- Lifetime or annual option for early adopters.

Teams:

- Seat-based annual plan.
- Team progress dashboard.
- Private cohorts.
- Custom package recommendations.

Institutions:

- Course licenses.
- Instructor dashboards.
- Assignment export.

## 10. Messaging Guardrails

Do:

- Emphasize effort, depth, and judgment.
- Show real stage examples.
- Make failed branches visible.
- Use famous papers as concrete anchors.
- Show that experts crafted the package.

Do not:

- Promise to make research easy.
- Describe the product as a generic AI tutor.
- Overuse "Duolingo for X" externally; it signals lightweight repetition.
- Lead with automated paper conversion; it weakens the expert-quality moat.
- Make the first impression feel like a passive reading app.

## 11. First Landing Page Structure

Hero:

- "Rebuild the research behind famous AI papers."
- Supporting line about decisions, implementation, experiments, evidence, and writing.
- Primary CTA: "Try the first research decision."

Section 1: The loop

- Choose a paper.
- Make the research decision.
- Implement or design the experiment.
- Run tests.
- Compare against expert branches.

Section 2: Example package

- Use one flagship paper.
- Show the decision graph and one failed branch.

Section 3: What you train

- Research framing.
- Math and implementation.
- Experiment design.
- Evidence interpretation.
- Research writing.

Section 4: Why this is different

- Paper summaries show the final story.
- ResearchCrafters exposes the journey.

Section 5: Pricing or waitlist

- Keep the CTA direct.

## 12. Core Narrative

The pitch should not be "we help you read papers." The stronger story is:

Modern AI progress is moving fast, and everyone has access to the same models and coding
assistants. The scarce skill is judgment: choosing what to try, understanding why an
experiment failed, and knowing what evidence actually supports a claim. ResearchCrafters
turns famous papers into executable case studies so engineers can practice that judgment
directly.
